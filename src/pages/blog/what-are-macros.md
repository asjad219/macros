# What Are Macros? A Complete Beginner Guide

"Macros" is short for macronutrients — the three major categories of nutrients that provide energy (calories): **protein**, **carbohydrates**, and **fat**. Every food you eat contains some combination of these three. Tracking macros means paying attention to how many grams of each you consume, not just total calories.

## Why Track Macros Instead of Just Calories?

Two people eating 2,000 calories per day can have completely different body composition outcomes depending on how those calories are distributed. 2,000 calories of mostly carbohydrates triggers different hormonal responses, muscle protein synthesis rates, and fat storage patterns than 2,000 calories balanced across protein, carbs, and fat.

Tracking macros gives you precision that calorie counting alone cannot. You can hit your calorie target while eating almost no protein — technically "on plan" but losing muscle mass and feeling terrible. Macros prevent this.

## Protein — The Most Important Macro

**Calories per gram: 4 kcal/g**

Protein is the only macronutrient that directly builds and repairs muscle tissue. It also:

- Has the highest thermic effect of food (TEF) — your body burns 20–30% of protein calories just digesting it
- Is the most satiating macronutrient — keeps you full longer than carbs or fat at the same calorie count
- Preserves muscle mass during a calorie deficit — critical for sustainable fat loss

**How much:** 1.6–2.2g per kg of bodyweight per day is the evidence-based range for active people. Prioritize hitting your protein target above all other macros.

## Carbohydrates — Your Body's Preferred Fuel

**Calories per gram: 4 kcal/g**

Carbohydrates are your body's preferred energy source, particularly for high-intensity exercise and brain function. They're stored in muscle and liver as glycogen — a readily available fuel tank.

Carbs are not inherently fattening. Excess calories cause fat gain, regardless of source. However, highly processed, rapidly digested carbs (white bread, sugary drinks) cause blood sugar spikes that promote hunger and fat storage more than complex carbs (oats, sweet potato, rice).

**How much:** Varies significantly by diet style. Standard balanced macros allocate 40–50% of calories to carbs. Low-carb diets drop to 20–25%. Keto drops to under 5%.

## Fat — Essential, Not the Enemy

**Calories per gram: 9 kcal/g**

Fat is calorie-dense but essential. Your body requires dietary fat for:

- Absorbing fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K)
- Producing hormones, including testosterone and estrogen
- Cell membrane integrity
- Brain function (60% of the brain is fat)

Fat was demonized from the 1970s–1990s based on flawed research. The science is clear: dietary fat does not cause obesity — excess calories do.

**How much:** 25–35% of total calories for most people. Essential fatty acids (omega-3 and omega-6) must come from diet — your body cannot synthesize them.

## How to Calculate Your Macros

The quickest way is to use our [TDEE and Macro Calculator](/). It calculates your Total Daily Energy Expenditure, applies your goal (cut, maintain, or bulk), and distributes calories across protein, carbs, and fat based on your chosen diet style.

The underlying math:

1. Calculate TDEE (BMR × activity multiplier)
2. Adjust for goal (subtract ~300–500 kcal for fat loss, add ~150–300 for muscle gain)
3. Set protein at 1.6–2.0g/kg bodyweight
4. Fill remaining calories with carbs and fat in your preferred ratio

## Do You Need to Track Macros Forever?

No. Tracking macros is a tool, not a lifestyle requirement. Most people track for 3–6 months to develop a clear intuition of what different foods contain. After that, many people maintain results with a rough understanding of their macros without weighing food daily.

The goal of tracking is to build nutritional literacy — understanding what you're actually eating — not to be tethered to a food scale indefinitely.
