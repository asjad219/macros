# What Is TDEE? The Complete Beginner's Guide to Total Daily Energy Expenditure

Before you can lose fat, build muscle, or even maintain your current weight with any consistency, you need to know one number: your TDEE.

TDEE stands for Total Daily Energy Expenditure. It is the total number of calories your body burns in a 24-hour period. Everything you eat either falls below, at, or above this number — and that relationship determines whether you lose weight, gain weight, or stay the same.

This guide explains exactly what TDEE is, how it is calculated, what affects it, and how to use it to reach your goal.

## What Does TDEE Mean?

Your TDEE is your personal calorie maintenance number. Eat exactly this many calories and your weight stays stable. Eat less and you lose weight. Eat more and you gain weight.

It sounds simple because it is. The challenge is that your TDEE is not a fixed number. It changes based on your body weight, activity level, age, and even how long you have been dieting. That is why understanding the components of TDEE matters — not just the final number.

## The Four Components of TDEE

Your total daily energy expenditure is made up of four separate components that add together to give you your full daily calorie burn.

### 1. BMR — Basal Metabolic Rate (60–70% of TDEE)

BMR is the largest component of your TDEE. It represents the calories your body burns at complete rest — the energy required just to keep your heart beating, your lungs breathing, your brain functioning, and your cells operating.

Even if you lay completely still in bed for 24 hours and did nothing at all, your body would still burn roughly this many calories just to stay alive.

Your BMR is primarily determined by:
- Body weight (more mass = higher BMR)
- Height (taller people burn more)
- Age (BMR decreases roughly 1–2% per decade after age 20)
- Sex (males typically have higher BMR due to greater muscle mass)
- Muscle mass (muscle burns more calories than fat at rest)

For most people, BMR accounts for 60–70% of their total daily calorie burn. This is why severe calorie restriction is counterproductive — eating below your BMR for extended periods causes muscle loss, which lowers your BMR, which makes fat loss harder over time.

### 2. TEF — Thermic Effect of Food (8–15% of TDEE)

Every time you eat, your body expends energy to digest, absorb, and metabolize that food. This is called the thermic effect of food.

Different macronutrients have very different thermic effects:
- Protein: 20–30% of its calories are burned during digestion
- Carbohydrates: 5–10% of its calories are burned during digestion
- Fat: 0–3% of its calories are burned during digestion

This is one reason high-protein diets are effective for fat loss — a significant portion of protein calories are burned just processing the food. Eating 200 calories of chicken breast results in a meaningfully different net calorie intake than eating 200 calories of butter.

### 3. EAT — Exercise Activity Thermogenesis (0–15% of TDEE)

EAT refers to the calories burned during intentional exercise — running, weight training, cycling, swimming, and any other planned physical activity.

For most people who exercise moderately (3–5 times per week), EAT accounts for roughly 10–15% of TDEE. For sedentary people, it can be close to zero.

This is why exercise alone is rarely enough to create meaningful fat loss — it represents a smaller portion of total calorie burn than most people expect. You cannot out-train a bad diet. But exercise does meaningfully raise your TDEE and has enormous benefits for body composition, health, and long-term weight maintenance.

### 4. NEAT — Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis (15–30% of TDEE)

NEAT is the most underappreciated component of TDEE. It represents all the calories you burn through physical movement that is not intentional exercise — walking to your car, fidgeting, typing, standing, doing household chores, taking the stairs.

NEAT varies enormously between individuals — by as much as 2,000 calories per day. A construction worker and a desk worker of the same height and weight can have dramatically different TDEEs, primarily due to NEAT.

NEAT is also highly adaptive. When you reduce calories, your body naturally reduces NEAT — you move less, fidget less, and unconsciously become more sedentary. This is a key driver of weight loss plateaus. Maintaining daily step count (10,000 steps is a reasonable target) helps protect NEAT while dieting.

## How TDEE Is Calculated

TDEE is calculated in two steps:

**Step 1: Calculate BMR**

The most accurate formula for the general population is the Mifflin-St Jeor equation:

For men: BMR = (10 × weight in kg) + (6.25 × height in cm) − (5 × age) + 5

For women: BMR = (10 × weight in kg) + (6.25 × height in cm) − (5 × age) − 161

**Step 2: Multiply by Activity Factor**

| Activity Level | Description | Multiplier |
|---|---|---|
| Sedentary | Desk job, little or no exercise | 1.2 |
| Lightly active | Light exercise 1–3 days per week | 1.375 |
| Moderately active | Exercise 3–5 days per week | 1.55 |
| Very active | Hard exercise 6–7 days per week | 1.725 |
| Extremely active | Physical job plus daily training | 1.9 |

TDEE = BMR × Activity Multiplier

For example: A 30-year-old woman, 165cm, 65kg, who exercises 3–4 times per week:

BMR = (10 × 65) + (6.25 × 165) − (5 × 30) − 161 = 650 + 1031 − 150 − 161 = 1,370 calories

TDEE = 1,370 × 1.55 = **2,124 calories**

## How to Use Your TDEE

Once you know your TDEE, using it is straightforward:

**To lose fat:** Eat 10–20% below your TDEE. A 500-calorie daily deficit creates roughly 1 lb of fat loss per week. Never go below your BMR.

**To build muscle:** Eat 10–15% above your TDEE. A small surplus provides the extra energy needed for muscle protein synthesis without excessive fat gain.

**To maintain weight:** Eat at your TDEE. This is your calorie maintenance level.

**For body recomposition:** Eat at or very near your TDEE with very high protein intake and consistent resistance training. Progress is slower but body composition improves simultaneously.

## What Affects Your TDEE Over Time

Your TDEE is not static. These factors change it:

**Body weight changes.** As you lose weight, your BMR decreases because there is less body mass to maintain. This is why calorie intake must be adjusted every 4–6 weeks during a diet. What created a deficit at 90kg may be maintenance at 80kg.

**Metabolic adaptation.** During prolonged calorie restriction, your body adapts by reducing BMR beyond what weight loss alone would explain. This is sometimes called "metabolic slowdown" and is a key reason diets become harder over time.

**Muscle mass changes.** Building muscle increases your BMR, which raises your TDEE. This is one of the long-term benefits of resistance training — a more muscular body burns more calories at rest.

**Age.** TDEE naturally decreases with age, primarily due to declining muscle mass (sarcopenia). Regular resistance training significantly slows this decline.

**Diet breaks.** Taking 1–2 weeks at maintenance calories during a prolonged cut can partially reverse metabolic adaptation and make the subsequent deficit phase more effective.

## How Accurate Is a TDEE Calculator?

TDEE calculators are accurate within roughly 10% for most people. For a person with a calculated TDEE of 2,500 calories, the true value likely falls between 2,250 and 2,750 calories.

This is accurate enough to be a reliable starting point. The practical approach is:

1. Calculate your TDEE
2. Set your calorie target based on your goal
3. Track your weight daily for 2–3 weeks
4. If weight is not changing as expected, adjust calories by 100–200 per day
5. Recalculate every 4–6 weeks or after significant weight change

## TDEE vs. BMR — What Is the Difference?

BMR is what your body burns at complete rest. TDEE is what your body burns in a real day, including all activity. TDEE is always higher than BMR.

You should never eat below your BMR for extended periods. Doing so for any length of time leads to muscle loss, nutrient deficiencies, and metabolic adaptation that makes fat loss harder.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How often should I recalculate my TDEE?**
Recalculate every 4–6 weeks, or whenever your body weight changes by more than 5 lbs (2.3 kg). As your weight changes, your TDEE changes too.

**Why is my weight not changing even at a deficit?**
Common causes include underestimating food intake, overestimating activity level, metabolic adaptation, or water retention masking fat loss. Verify your tracking accuracy first, then consider a 100-calorie further reduction.

**Should I eat back calories burned during exercise?**
This depends on how you chose your activity multiplier. If you chose "moderately active" and exercise is already factored in, do not eat back exercise calories. If you chose "sedentary" and exercise on top of that, eating back a portion (50–75%) of exercise calories is reasonable.

**Is TDEE the same every day?**
No. Your TDEE fluctuates daily based on activity, sleep, stress, and other factors. The calculated TDEE is an average. Hitting your weekly calorie target is more important than hitting it perfectly every single day.
