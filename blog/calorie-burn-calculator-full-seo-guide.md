<a name="bmr-vs-tdee"></a>
## 1. BMR vs. TDEE: What's the Difference?

Two numbers get thrown around constantly, and mixing them up is the #1 reason people miscalculate their calorie needs.

**BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate)** is the energy your body burns at total rest — no movement, no digestion, just organs functioning. Your heart pumping, lungs breathing, brain firing, cells regenerating. If you literally stayed in bed all day, this is roughly what you'd burn.

**TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure)** is your BMR plus everything layered on top of it:

- Exercise (formal workouts, sports, cardio)
- NEAT — Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis (walking, fidgeting, chores, standing)
- TEF — Thermic Effect of Food (calories burned digesting what you eat, roughly 10% of intake)

TDEE is the number that actually matters for weight management, because it reflects your real day — not a hypothetical one spent motionless.

<a name="how-its-calculated"></a>
## 2. How Calorie Burn Is Actually Calculated

Our calculator uses the **Mifflin-St Jeor equation**, published in 1990 and widely regarded by dietitians as the most accurate BMR formula for the general population — more reliable than the older Harris-Benedict equation from 1919, which tends to overestimate.

The formula:

**For men:**
`BMR = (10 × weight in kg) + (6.25 × height in cm) − (5 × age) + 5`

**For women:**
`BMR = (10 × weight in kg) + (6.25 × height in cm) − (5 × age) − 161`

Once BMR is calculated, it's multiplied by an activity factor to produce TDEE:

`TDEE = BMR × Activity Multiplier`

That activity multiplier is where most generic calculators fall apart — they use vague labels without explaining what they mean. Here's the exact breakdown we use:

<a name="activity-multipliers"></a>
## 3. Activity Multipliers Explained

| Activity Level | Multiplier | What This Actually Looks Like |
|---|---|---|
| Sedentary | 1.2 | Desk job, little to no exercise |
| Lightly active | 1.375 | Light exercise 1–3 days/week |
| Moderately active | 1.55 | Moderate exercise 3–5 days/week |
| Very active | 1.725 | Hard exercise 6–7 days/week |
| Extremely active | 1.9 | Physical job + daily intense training |

Most people overestimate their own activity level. Someone who works a desk job and hits the gym 3x a week is usually "lightly active," not "moderately active" — because the other 5-6 days involve minimal movement outside the gym session. Picking the wrong tier is the single most common reason calorie calculators give inflated numbers.

<a name="what-affects-it"></a>
## 4. What Affects Your Calorie Burn

Your daily burn shifts based on several real, measurable factors:

- **Body weight and composition** — muscle tissue is more metabolically active than fat, so two people at the same weight can have different BMRs depending on body composition.
- **Age** — metabolic rate declines gradually with age, driven largely by natural loss of muscle mass over time rather than age itself.
- **Height and sex** — taller individuals and men typically have higher BMRs due to greater lean body mass on average.
- **Activity level** — the largest lever you actually control day to day.
- **NEAT** — incidental movement (walking, standing, fidgeting, chores) that varies enormously between individuals and can account for hundreds of calories of difference per day, even between people with similar exercise habits.
- **Sleep and stress** — chronic sleep deprivation and elevated cortisol are linked to metabolic changes and altered appetite regulation, indirectly affecting energy balance.

<a name="using-your-number"></a>
## 5. How to Use Your Number for Your Goal

Once you have your TDEE, the strategy is straightforward:

**Fat loss:** Eat 300–500 calories below TDEE for a sustainable rate of roughly 0.5–1 lb per week. Avoid deficits larger than 750–1000 calories unless under medical supervision — aggressive cuts accelerate muscle loss and metabolic slowdown.

**Maintenance:** Eat at or near your TDEE, adjusting slightly as your weight trends up or down over 2-3 weeks.

**Muscle gain:** Eat 200–300 calories above TDEE, paired with progressive resistance training, to favor lean mass gain over excess fat storage.

A practical approach: start at your calculated target, track your weight trend (not day-to-day fluctuations) for two to three weeks, and adjust by 100–150 calories if the trend doesn't match your goal.

<a name="common-mistakes"></a>
## 6. Common Mistakes People Make

- **Cutting too aggressively.** A 1000+ calorie deficit feels faster but usually backfires — increased hunger, muscle loss, and rebound weight regain.
- **Not recalculating after weight change.** TDEE drops as you lose weight and rises as you gain. A number calculated at 200 lbs won't hold at 170 lbs.
- **Overestimating activity level.** Choosing "very active" because you "feel busy" rather than because your actual exercise volume supports it.
- **Ignoring NEAT.** Two people with identical workouts can have very different TDEEs if one sits all day and the other walks 10,000+ steps.
- **Trusting a single day's scale weight.** Water retention, sodium, and hormonal fluctuations can swing daily weight by 2–4 lbs. Use a weekly average instead.

<a name="accuracy"></a>
## 7. How Accurate Is This Calculator?

The Mifflin-St Jeor equation is generally accurate within about 10% for most people without significant muscle mass outliers (very lean athletes or individuals with obesity may see larger deviations). Treat your result as a **starting point**, not a fixed law — then calibrate against 2-3 weeks of real-world weight tracking and adjust from there.

<a name="faq"></a>
## 8. FAQ

**How many calories do I burn a day just sitting?**
That's essentially your BMR plus minimal NEAT — for most adults this falls somewhere between 1,200 and 2,000 calories depending on body size, age, and sex.

**Does the calculator include exercise?**
Yes, through the activity multiplier you select. If your training volume changes meaningfully, recalculate to keep your TDEE accurate.

**Why do two calculators give me different numbers?**
Different sites use different formulas (Mifflin-St Jeor vs. Harris-Benedict vs. Katch-McArdle) and different activity multiplier scales. Small formula differences compound, so results can vary by 100–200 calories between tools.

**How often should I recalculate my TDEE?**
Every 5-10 lbs of weight change, or every 4-6 weeks during an active cut or bulk.

**Is this the same as calories burned during a workout?**
No — that's a separate, workout-specific calculation. TDEE is your full-day total, which already factors in a general activity level rather than isolating one session.
